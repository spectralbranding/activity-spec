# Historical Notes: Sources and Accuracy

This document records the historical basis for each demo specification. All dates, institutional contexts, and biographical details are drawn from published sources.

---

## Claude Shannon (December 1946)

**Primary sources**:
- Soni, J. & Goodman, R. (2017). *A Mind at Play: How Claude Shannon Invented the Information Age*. Simon & Schuster.
- Shannon, C.E. (1938). "A Symbolic Analysis of Relay and Switching Circuits." Master's thesis, MIT.
- Shannon, C.E. (1948). "A Mathematical Theory of Communication." *Bell System Technical Journal*, 27(3), 379-423; 27(4), 623-656.
- Shannon, C.E. (1949). "Communication Theory of Secrecy Systems." *Bell System Technical Journal*, 28(4), 656-715. (Written ~1945, classified until 1949.)

**Key dates**:
- 1916: Born in Petoskey, Michigan
- 1936: BS Mathematics and Electrical Engineering, University of Michigan
- 1937: MS Electrical Engineering, MIT (Boolean algebra thesis)
- 1940: PhD Mathematics, Princeton (genetics application)
- 1941: Joined Bell Labs (mathematical research)
- 1943-1945: Classified cryptography work
- 1948: "A Mathematical Theory of Communication" published in BSTJ

**Spec date justification (December 1946)**: Shannon had completed his wartime cryptography work and was back at Bell Labs doing open research. The information theory paper was gestating but not yet written. His 1937 thesis (Boolean algebra = relay circuits) was the established methodological pattern. This is the moment of maximum potential before the breakthrough.

**Intellectual priors verification**:
- Boole: Shannon explicitly credited Boole in his 1937 thesis
- Nyquist: Bell Labs colleague; "Certain Factors Affecting Telegraph Speed" (1924)
- Hartley: Bell Labs; "Transmission of Information" (1928) — Shannon cited directly
- Boltzmann: Shannon's entropy formula H = -sum(p*log(p)) is formally identical to Boltzmann's entropy; Shannon acknowledged this in the 1948 paper

## Gustav Fechner (February 1855)

**Primary sources**:
- Fechner, G.T. (1860). *Elemente der Psychophysik*. Breitkopf und Hartel, Leipzig.
- Boring, E.G. (1950). *A History of Experimental Psychology*. 2nd ed. Appleton-Century-Crofts. Chapter 14.
- Heidelberger, M. (2004). *Nature from Within: Gustav Theodor Fechner and His Psychophysical Worldview*. University of Pittsburgh Press.

**Key dates**:
- 1801: Born in Gross Sarchen, Lusatia
- 1817: Enrolled at University of Leipzig (medicine, then physics)
- 1834: Appointed Professor of Physics, Leipzig
- 1839-1843: Severe illness and blindness; resigned professorship
- 1850, October 22: The "morning insight" — logarithmic psychophysical law
- 1855-1859: Systematic experimental work on thresholds
- 1860: *Elemente der Psychophysik* published

**Spec date justification (February 1855)**: Fechner had the foundational insight (October 22, 1850) and had been accumulating experimental data for 4+ years. The book was still 5 years away. This is mid-experimental-program — the work is real but unpublished.

**Intellectual priors verification**:
- Weber: Fechner's colleague at Leipzig; Weber's law (JND proportional to stimulus) published 1834
- Leibniz: Fechner explicitly cited Leibniz's monads and continuity principle
- Herbart: "Psychologie als Wissenschaft" (1824-1825) — first attempt at mathematical psychology
- Bernoulli: Fechner noted the parallel between his logarithmic law and Bernoulli's utility theory (1738)

**Unusual case note**: Fechner's "I need" spec represents a field that doesn't exist. Psychophysics was named and defined by Fechner himself. There was no organization seeking this capability — Fechner created both the question and the methodology to answer it.

## W. Edwards Deming (April 1947)

**Primary sources**:
- Deming, W.E. (1986). *Out of the Crisis*. MIT Press.
- Gabor, A. (1990). *The Man Who Discovered Quality*. Times Books.
- JUSE (Union of Japanese Scientists and Engineers). Historical records of Deming's 1950 lectures.

**Key dates**:
- 1900: Born in Sioux City, Iowa
- 1921: BS Engineering, University of Wyoming
- 1925: MS Mathematics and Physics, University of Colorado
- 1928: PhD Mathematical Physics, Yale
- 1927-1930s: Studied with Shewhart at Bell Labs (Hawthorne plant)
- 1939: Joined U.S. Census Bureau
- 1942-1945: Taught statistical quality control to wartime manufacturers
- 1947: First trip to Japan (census consultation)
- 1950: JUSE lectures on statistical quality control
- 1951: Deming Prize established in Japan

**Spec date justification (April 1947)**: Deming had wartime quality teaching experience and census expertise. The Japan quality lectures were 3 years away. American industry was ignoring statistical methods in the postwar boom. This is the moment of frustration — the methodology exists but has no willing audience.

**Intellectual priors verification**:
- Shewhart: Deming studied directly under Shewhart; edited "Statistical Method from the Viewpoint of Quality Control" (1939)
- Fisher: Deming's census sampling work drew on Fisher's experimental design
- C.I. Lewis: Deming repeatedly cited Lewis's operational definitions ("Mind and the World Order," 1929)

## Herbert Simon (September 1952)

**Primary sources**:
- Simon, H.A. (1991). *Models of My Life*. Basic Books. (Autobiography)
- Simon, H.A. (1947). *Administrative Behavior*. Macmillan.
- Crowther-Heyck, H. (2005). *Herbert A. Simon: The Bounds of Reason in Modern America*. Johns Hopkins University Press.

**Key dates**:
- 1916: Born in Milwaukee, Wisconsin
- 1936: BA Political Science, University of Chicago
- 1943: PhD Political Science, University of Chicago
- 1947: *Administrative Behavior* published (revised dissertation)
- 1949: Moved to Carnegie Tech GSIA
- 1952: At Carnegie, beginning formalization of bounded rationality
- 1955-1956: Logic Theorist developed with Newell and Shaw (at RAND)
- 1957: *Models of Man* published
- 1975: Turing Award
- 1978: Nobel Prize in Economics

**Spec date justification (September 1952)**: Simon had recently arrived at Carnegie Tech GSIA. "Administrative Behavior" was published but bounded rationality was not yet formalized mathematically. The Logic Theorist was 4 years away. This is the interdisciplinary synthesis moment — political science + economics + psychology + early computation.

**Intellectual priors verification**:
- Barnard: Simon cited Barnard's "Functions of the Executive" (1938) extensively in "Administrative Behavior"
- von Neumann: Simon engaged directly with game theory and rejected the perfect rationality assumption
- Whitehead & Russell: Simon's later work on Logic Theorist was a direct attempt to mechanize Principia reasoning

## Taiichi Ohno (October 1948)

**Primary sources**:
- Ohno, T. (1978/1988). *Toyota Production System: Beyond Large-Scale Production*. Productivity Press.
- Shimokawa, K. & Fujimoto, T. (2009). *The Birth of Lean*. Lean Enterprise Institute.
- Liker, J.K. (2004). *The Toyota Way*. McGraw-Hill.

**Key dates**:
- 1912: Born in Dalian, Manchuria (then Japanese-controlled)
- 1932: Graduated Nagoya Technical High School
- 1932: Joined Toyoda Spinning and Weaving (family textile company)
- 1943: Transferred to Toyota Motor Company
- 1947-1948: Became machine shop manager at Honsha plant
- 1948-1975: Progressive development of Toyota Production System
- 1978: *Toyota Production System* published

**Spec date justification (October 1948)**: Ohno was newly appointed as machine shop manager. Toyota was in crisis (near bankruptcy, Kiichiro Toyoda's 3-year challenge). The supermarket pull-system insight was crystallizing (the exact timing is debated — some sources place the supermarket observation during a 1950s visit to the US, others say earlier via publications). TPS was embryonic. This is the inception moment.

**Intellectual priors verification**:
- Ford: Ohno explicitly referenced Ford's flow production principles, then explained why he rejected Ford's batch-and-push model for Toyota's context
- Toyoda Sakichi: The jidoka principle (automation with human intelligence) from the automatic loom is directly credited by Ohno
- American supermarkets: Ohno's own writings describe the supermarket as the inspiration for kanban

**Note on the supermarket timeline**: The exact date of Ohno's supermarket insight is contested. Some biographers place it during a physical visit to the US in the 1950s; others argue Ohno learned about American supermarkets from publications or colleagues earlier. The YAML spec acknowledges this ambiguity.
